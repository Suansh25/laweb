T E L A N G A N A

తెలంగాణ  TELANGANA

‘South of North and North of South,’ Telangana has long been a meeting place for diverse languages and cultures. It is easily the best example for India’s composite culture, pluralism and inclusiveness. Located on the uplands of Deccan plateau, Telangana is the link between the North and South of India. It is thus no surprise that the region on the whole came to be known for its Ganga-Jamuna Tehzeeb and the capital Hyderabad as a ‘miniature India!’ The state is home to several tribal communities, each with their own languages, such as Gondi, Kolami, Lambadi, Chenchu, and Koya.

తెలంగాణ  అద్భుతాలు...

Telangana, a state in southern India, is a vibrant tapestry of linguistic diversity. The official language is Telugu, spoken by about 76% of the population1. Telugu, with its rich literary heritage, has several dialects within the state, reflecting regional variations2. Urdu is the second official language, spoken by around 12% of the population1. Historically, Urdu was the official language of the Hyderabad State before 1948, and it remains widely used, especially in Hyderabad1. In addition to Telugu and Urdu, Telangana is home to several tribal languages, including Gondi, Lambadi, and Kolami3. These languages are spoken by various indigenous communities, each with its own unique cultural and linguistic traditions3. Hindi and English are also commonly spoken, particularly in urban areas3. The linguistic landscape of Telangana is a reflection of its historical and cultural amalgamation. The region’s history of diverse rulers, including the Satavahanas, Kakatiyas, Qutub Shahis, and Asaf Jahis, has contributed to its rich cultural and linguistic heritage

Telangana's famous artworks

Perini Sivatandavam

Perini Sivatandavam, also known as ‘dance of warriors’, the commoners, faced with the challenges of daily life developed traditions of story-telling coupled with solutions to tide over them through Golla Suddulu, Oggu Kathalu and Gotralu etc.

Kolami Language

Kolami (Northwestern Kolami/Southeastern Kolami) is a tribal Central Dravidian language spoken in Maharashtra and Telangana states of India. It falls under the Kolami–Naiki group of languages. It is the most widely spoken Central Dravidian language.

Koya Language

Koya is a South-Central Dravidian language of the Gondi–Kui group spoken in central and southern India. It is the native language of the Koya people. It is sometimes described as a dialect of Gondi, but it is mutually unintelligible with Gondi dialects.

Lambadi Language

Lambadi, Lambani, Lamani or Banjari is a Western Indo-Aryan language spoken by the Banjara people across India. The language does not have a native script. Regional dialects are divided between the Banjara of Maharashtra (written in Devanagari), Karnataka (written in the Kannada script), and Telangana (written in the Telugu script). .

Bidri Craft

The unique art of silver engraved on metal. Black, gold and silver coatings are applied on this. It involves various several stages like casting, engraving, inlaying and oxidizing. The name of this art form is derived from a town called Bidar (currently part of Karnataka) of the erstwhile Hyderabad state

Nirmal Art

The renowned Nirmal oil paintings use natural dyes for depicting themes from the epics such as Ramayana and Mahabharata. Also, the wood paintings and other wooden articles, has great aesthetic expression. The origin of the Nirmal craft is traced back to the Kakatiya era.

Qawali, Ghazals and Mushairas

Qawali, Ghazals and Mushairas evolved under the patronage of Qutub Shahi and the Asafjahi rulers in and around the capital city of Hyderabad.
